I.                  INTRODUCTION

 

 

When you enter into a federal contract or subcontract (or help fulfill either if over a certain dollar amount), you have additional obligations beyond the usual requirements all employers face under federal law. The intent of the obligations is to ensure that those who the government does business with follow high standards of conduct in their business dealings, including in their treatment of employees. Most of the obligations overlap requirements you already have under federal law as an employer, but by a being a federal contractor, you agree to take certain actions to comply, and to give the government additional oversight and remedies for violations. The obligations apply to your conduct with all employees who work on the contract and in all facilities used to fulfill the contract, and the equal opportunity/affirmative action requirements apply to all employees at all facilities (unless certain facilities or classes of employees are exempted in advance). There are five general areas of obligations on a federal contractor, as an employer:

 

 

  1. Employee Eligibility to Work in the United States (E-Verify)
  2. Wage and Hour
  3. Ethical Conduct by Contractors and Employees
  4. Labor (Union)
  5. Equal Opportunity/Affirmative Action

 

 

The following sections discuss the contractual obligations assumed by a federal contractor or subcontractor as an employer, the conditions that trigger specific obligations (type of contract, dollar value of contract), and the remedies for violations. Please note that unless otherwise specified, any reference below to a “contractor” includes both a contractor and a subcontractor.

 

The contractual obligations arise from a set of laws and executive orders[2] (referred to in this document individually as the “Law” and collectively as the “Laws”) mandating that government contracting agencies and in turn, contractors hired by these agencies, include specific clauses in each federal contract, and at times subcontracts, recognizing the applicability of the Laws. Each contract must have, in other words, an agreement to comply with the Laws (referred to herein as a “compliance clause”). The compliance clauses explain the requirements applicable to the contractor and are provided in their entirety in Section IV of this document.

 

If a subcontract requires a compliance clause, then all purchase orders under that subcontract also require reference to the compliance clause or to a separate document which references it. Even if not made under a subcontract, a purchase order, if it also meets the dollar threshold for subcontracts under the applicable Law, must reference the compliance clause or a separate document which references it. For instructions on referencing compliance clauses, see the introduction to Section IV.B.

 

The Laws and associated regulations are primarily enforced by either the contracting agency or divisions within the Department of Labor (DOL) - the Wage and Hour Division (WHD) and the Office of Federal Contract Compliance Programs (OFCCP). The federal agencies’ oversight roles include verifying that the contractor meets the requirements of the Laws in fulfilling the contract or subcontract. The agencies accomplish this through random audits and by responding to individual complaints.[3]

 

Though employees may complain of violations, only the government is entitled to initiate legal action to enforce the contractual requirements. Conversely, the Laws do not interfere with an employee’s right to bring legal action under a parallel federal law protecting the same rights, for example, Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964’s protections against discrimination in employment. Put another way, while both sets of laws are for the employee’s benefit, the Laws that apply to federal contractors grant, and provide for enforcement of, rights that belong to the government; whereas other federal laws that apply to all employers grant, and provide for enforcement of, rights that essentially belong to employees.

 

To remedy violations, the agencies can reach an agreement with a contractor to correct a problem, or can bring legal action in court. The consequences contractors face for violations include paying damages to affected employees, having payments due under the contract withheld (while possibly still being required to perform under the contract), fines, having the contract cancelled and being barred from future federal contracts. The Obama administration has made enforcing these Laws a priority, and the agencies’ (particularly the OFCCP, which enforces equal opportunity/affirmative action requirements) attention to federal contract compliance is growing in scope and frequency.

 

The requirements with which contractors must comply can be divided into four general categories:

 

A.    agreeing as part of the federal contract or subcontract to comply with federal contractor employer requirements, as well as including the compliance clause in all subcontracts covered under the applicable Law

B.     providing employees (and unions if applicable) notice of their rights and responsibilities (posters, notices) under the Laws and in some cases developing and implementing policies to educate and monitor employees regarding their responsibilities

C.     maintaining records required by the Laws for review by federal agencies

D.    reporting required information to agencies

 

This document describes each of the requirements for each Law.

 

Special Note on “Commercial Items”

 

The Federal Acquisition Streamlining Act of 1994 established a requirement on federal agencies to give preference to acquiring supplies and services for government use which already exist in the commercial marketplace, and at the same time simplified the acquisition methods for acquiring those items. Several of the Laws discussed herein provide exemptions for contracts for “commercial items,” and/or two subcategories of “commercial items” which are “commercially available off-the-shelf items” (a “commercial item” which is widely and readily available) and “commercially available services” (services related to a “commercial item” or services for any item, which service is commonly available in the commercial marketplace). The government’s definitions of these items are discussed in Sections III.2.A & III.2.B, herein, and where applicable, the exemptions for commercial supplies or services are noted in the discussion of each Law in Section III, herein.

1.     EMPLOYEE ELIGIBILITY TO WORK IN THE UNITED STATES (E-VERIFY)

 

 

All employers are required to maintain proof of eligibility to work in the U.S. for all of their employees (Form I-9). In addition, Executive Order 12989 (as amended by Executive Order 13465 [2008]) requires certain federal contractors and subcontractors to use the federal E-Verify system of electronic work eligibility verification provided by the Department of Homeland Security - U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS) and the Social Security Administration (SSA). E-Verify is required for contractors with a prime contract that meets the following four criteria:

 

v          entered into or modified after September 8, 2009

v          in an amount greater than $150,000

v          which lasts 120 days or longer

v          with at least some of the work in fulfillment of the contract being performed in the U.S.

 

 

Prime contractors providing supplies (or materials) and services must follow E-Verify requirements. Contracts for “commercially available off-the-shelf items” (and items that would qualify but for minor modifications) and “commercially available services” related to those same products and, if performed by the vendor, are exempted from the requirements (see definition of “commercial items,” and two subsets of “commercial items,” which are “commercially available off-the-shelf items” and “commercially available services” in Section III.2.A, immediately following, below).

 

Also subject to the requirements are contractors with a subcontract that is necessary for fulfilling a prime contract with an E-Verify clause and that meets the following four criteria:

 

v          entered into or modified after September 8, 2009

v          for service (and, not related to service of “commercially available off-the-shelf items,” performed by the vendor, and normally provided for that item) - [supply or materials subcontracts are exempt]

v          in an amount greater than $3,000[4]

v          with at least some of the work in fulfillment of the contract being performed in the U.S.

 

 

A prime contractor must include the E-Verify compliance clause in all covered service subcontracts, and a copy of the clause and citation for inclusion by reference appear in Section IV.B.1 herein. Additionally, a prime contractor is responsible for the following requirements by its subcontractors - that they in turn include the compliance clause in their own covered subcontracts and that they enroll in and use E-Verify. Contractors who use E-Verify must post two employee notices - E-Verify Participation Poster and the Office of Special Counsel’s Right to Work Poster. Both posters must be displayed prominently in areas accessible to applicants and employees, and posted in English and Spanish. When registering for E-Verify, the employer is prompted to download both posters.

 

E-Verify is required for all employees who work directly on the contract or subcontract in the U.S. When subject to E-Verify for the first time, a contractor has 30 days to enroll and 90 days after enrollment to begin verifying new and existing employees working on a contract. Once enrolled for 90 days, a contractor must verify all new hires assigned to work on the contract or subcontract within 3 business days of hire, and must verify existing employees, and not already verified, within 30 calendar days of the employee being assigned to work on the contract or subcontract. Instead of tracking employees used to fulfill a covered contract or subcontract, the contractor can choose to begin verifying all existing (subject to the exceptions below) and newly hired employees, and if choosing that, has 180 days from enrolling or of notifying USCIS of its intention to verify all employees to begin doing so.

 

Employees who normally perform support work, such as general company administration or indirect or overhead functions, and who do not perform substantial duties applicable to an individual contract are exempted, but may be voluntarily submitted to E-Verify. Any employees who have been employed continuously by the contractor since November 5, 1986 or earlier and any employees previously confirmed as authorized through E-Verify are exempt and may not be (re-)submitted to E-Verify.

 

Participation in E-Verify does not exempt contractors from the responsibility to complete, retain, and make available for inspection Forms I-9 that relate to its employees (and in fact DHS is escalating its inspection and enforcement of I-9 compliance), or exempt them from other requirements of applicable regulations or laws. However, the following modified requirements apply as part of the contractor's participation in E-Verify:

 

(1) Identity documents used for verification purposes must have photos (except with respect to certain accommodations).

 

(2) If a contractor obtains confirmation of the identity and employment eligibility of an individual in compliance with the terms and conditions of E-Verify, a presumption is established that the contractor has not violated section 274A(a)(1)(A) of the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA) with respect to the hiring of the individual.

 

(3) The contractor must notify DHS if it continues to employ any individual for whom the contractor has received a final E-Verify non-confirmation, and the contractor is subject to a civil penalty of between $500 and $1,000 for each failure to notify DHS of continued employment following a final non-confirmation.

 

(4) If a contractor continues to employ an individual after receiving a final E-Verify non-confirmation and that employee is subsequently found to be an unauthorized alien, the contractor is subject to a presumption that it has knowingly employed an unauthorized alien in violation of Immigration and Nationality Act (INA) section 274A(a).

 

(5) No person or entity participating in E-Verify is civilly or criminally liable to an employee under any law for any action taken in good faith reliance on information provided through the confirmation system (for example terminating the employment of an individual following a final non-confirmation).

Discrimination/Retaliation - The E-Verify MOU prohibits an employer from using E-Verify procedures for pre-employment screening of job applicants. In addition, an employer cannot verify only certain employees on the basis of perceived national origin. The Immigration Reform and Control Act of 1986, the law which created the obligation for employers to verify work eligibility of its employees, prohibits all employers with 4+ employees from discriminating against employees eligible to work in the U.S., on the basis of citizenship or national origin. The prohibition applies to persons who are legally present, permitted to work, and who “intend citizenship”- have applied for naturalization once enough time of residence has passed. It does not protect against discrimination against persons who have not applied for citizenship, does not prohibit an employer from giving preference to a U.S. citizen over a non-citizen if they are equally qualified, and does not apply to positions which require citizenship by law. The law further prohibits retaliation or intimidation against any person who complains of a perceived violation of this law or any employee who assists in another’s claim; and, includes document abuse provisions which prohibit an employer from asking an employee for more than the minimum documents required by the I-9 as a pre-text for citizenship discrimination. Additionally, a contractor agrees not to discriminate on the basis of national origin, or retaliate against an individual who claims a violation of this requirement, by being subject to Executive Order 11246 (discussed in Section III.5.A, below).

 

Personal Identification Verification - In addition to verifying work eligibility, contractors whose employees have access to government facilities or government information systems are subject to Homeland Security Presidential Directive (HSPD) 12-Policy for a Common Identification Standard for Federal Employees and Contractors (2004), which establishes safeguards for verifying the identity of and establishing secure identifications for federal and contractor employees. A separate compliance clause will be included in the solicitation and contract, and requires the contractor to include the clause in all subcontracts if the contract is covered by the Directive. 2.     WAGE AND HOUR OBLIGATIONS

 

 

Several Laws address wage and hour issues regarding federal supply and service contractors and primarily concern the minimum wages paid to employees.[5] The laws do not displace or supersede rights given to employees under any other law, including the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 (“FLSA”) or the Occupational Safety and Health Act of 1970 (“OSHA”) however, the wage and hour requirements as a condition of a contract apply only to employees working on the contract as defined below. The regulations for all of the wage and hour Laws state that a contractor will be jointly liable for any violations of its subcontractor(s) and any of their subcontractor(s), necessitating a contractor using the applicable compliance clause in any of its subcontracts that are also subject to the Law(s). A copy of the clauses and citations for inclusion by reference appear in Section IV.B.2, herein.

 

Note on False Claims under contract Wage and Hour Laws - In addition to any other remedies provided in the Laws below, false claims and “whistleblower” lawsuits apply to fraudulent (or just wrong if made with a “reckless disregard” for the truth of the information) requests for payment under a contract as a violation of the False Claims Act (discussed below in Section III.3.F). Additionally, a contractor can be liable under the False Claim Act for violations of its subcontractor(s) or their subcontractor(s). There are two primary sources of liability for a false claim under a federal wage and hour Law: 1. failure to pay overtime compensation properly, and 2. overbilling under a contract (under time-and-materials or labor-hour contracts) for hours actually worked.

 

This section provides wage and hour Law details for the following four categories of federal contracts:

 

A.    supply contracts of $15,000+ (minimum wage and overtime, child and convict labor)

B.     service contracts of $2,500+ (prevailing wages and benefits)

C.     service contracts of $2,500+ and supply contracts between $3,000 and $15,000 (convict labor)

D.    service contracts of $150,000+ (overtime and non-displacement of predecessor contractor employees)

 

 

A.    SUPPLY CONTRACTS OF $15,000+

 

 

Federal supply contractors with a contract of $15,000+, and not for “commercial items,” are subject to the Walsh-Healey Public Contracts Act of 1936 (or “PCA,” which preceded and served as a reference for the wage and hour law applicable to all U.S. employers, the Fair Labor Standards Act).

 

“Supply contractors” are those providing “the manufacturing or furnishing of materials, supplies, articles, and equipment in any amount exceeding $15,000.” This does not include goods generally available to the public in the open market (see discussion below regarding “commercial items”), nor does it apply to perishable and agricultural products, public utilities, periodicals, or those contracts or classes of contracts exempted by a federal agency. The PCA applies to subcontracts also above $15,000 that take on any of the responsibility of the prime contract to manufacture, provide, transport, and in some cases provide parts or equipment necessary for the machinery used in production of the supplies or materials in the prime contract.

 

The PCA requires the same minimum wage, hours of labor restrictions per week (40 hours) and overtime premiums (time and a half) for time worked in excess of 40 hours as the FLSA (and its regulations provide certain exceptions to the minimum wage requirements for apprentices, student-learners and certain handicapped workers consistent with the standards provided for in the FLSA and its regulations). The PCA prohibits the use of employees under 16 years of age and also employees who are currently incarcerated or imprisoned, under a work release program (subject to limited exceptions). No workplaces in which the materials are produced may be “unsanitary or hazardous or dangerous to the health and safety” of the employees. The safety standards are incorporated by OSHA regulations which apply to federal contractors the same as they do to any other employer. Additionally, the PCA regulations require the OSHA record-keeping requirements to be followed by the contractor. The compliance clause for contracts subject to Walsh-Healy is in Section IV.B.2.A, herein.

 

Required Notice - Supply contractors are required to post notice to employees regarding their rights under the PCA - WH Publication 1313. If the contractor employs workers with disabilities under special minimum wage certificates, then it also must post a special notice for those employees - WH Publication 1284.

 

Record-Keeping - Under the PCA regulations, records that must be retained for at least 2 years from the later of the date of their last entry or their last effective date are:

i.        basic employment and earnings records such as daily time cards, time sheets or records showing amounts of work accomplished

ii.      wage rate tables

iii.   work time schedules

Records that must be kept for at least 3 years from the date of their last entry are:

i.        name, address, sex and occupation

ii.      date of birth of each employee under 19 years of age. If the employer has obtained a certificate of age, the following must be recorded: the title and address of the office issuing the certificate; the number of the certificate; the date of issuance; the name, address and date of birth of the minor as appears on the certificate

iii.   wage and hour records, which include the wage rates and the amounts paid each pay period, the hours worked each day and each week, the period during which the employee worked on a government contract, and the number of the contract

 

Penalties - Penalties for violating the PCA are the differences in wages owed versus wages paid, as well as fines of $10/employee/day for underage or incarcerated employees. The penalty amounts may be withheld from payments due under the contract or the government may bring legal action to collect underpaid wages. Further, violations can result in termination of the contract (with costs of replacing the contractor being charged to the contractor) and prohibition from future government contract work for up to 3 years. Additionally, a contractor may be jointly liable for any violations of its subcontractor(s), and any of their subcontractor(s).

 

“Commercial Items” Exemption - When subcontracting for items commercially available to the general public, the compliance clause normally required in subcontracts under Walsh-Healey may not be applicable. The Federal Acquisition Regulations (“FAR”) Parts 12.503 and 12.504 exempt “commercial items” from Walsh-Healey prime contracts and subcontracts respectively; and FAR Part 2.101 defines a “commercial item” as

 

(1) any item, other than real property, that is of a type customarily used by the general public or by non-governmental entities for purposes other than governmental purposes, and -

(i) has been sold, leased, or licensed to the general public; or

 

(ii) has been offered for sale, lease, or license to the general public

 

(2) any item that evolved from an item described in paragraph (1) of this definition through advances in technology or performance and that is not yet available in the commercial marketplace, but will be available in the commercial marketplace in time to satisfy the delivery requirements under a government solicitation

 

(3) any item that would satisfy a criterion expressed in paragraphs (1) or (2) of this definition, but for -

 

(i) modifications of a type customarily available in the commercial marketplace; or

 

(ii) minor modifications of a type not customarily available in the commercial marketplace made to meet federal government requirements. “Minor modifications” means modifications that do not significantly alter the nongovernmental function or essential physical characteristics of an item or component, or change the purpose of a process. Factors to be considered in determining whether a modification is minor include the value and size of the modification and the comparative value and size of the final product. Dollar values and percentages may be used as guides, but are not conclusive evidence that a modification is minor.

 

(4) any combination of items meeting the requirements of paragraphs (1), (2), (3) or (5) of this definition that are of a type customarily combined and sold in combination to the general public

(5) installation services, maintenance services, repair services, training services, and other services if -

 

(i) such services are procured for support of an item referred to in paragraph (1), (2), (3) or (4) of this definition, regardless of whether such services are provided by the same source or at the same time as the item; and

 

(ii) the source of such services provides similar services contemporaneously to the general public under terms and conditions similar to those offered to the federal government

 

(6) services of a type offered and sold competitively in substantial quantities in the commercial marketplace based on established catalog or market prices for specific tasks performed or specific outcomes to be achieved and under standard commercial terms and conditions. For purposes of these services -

 

(i) “catalog price” means a price included in a catalog, price list, schedule, or other form that is regularly maintained by the manufacturer or vendor, is either published or otherwise available for inspection by customers, and states prices at which sales are currently, or were last, made to a significant number of buyers constituting the general public; and

 

(ii) “market prices” means current prices that are established in the course of ordinary trade between buyers and sellers free to bargain and that can be substantiated through competition or from sources independent of the offerors

 

(7) any item, combination of items, or service referred to in paragraphs (1) through (6) of this definition, notwithstanding the fact that the item, combination of items, or service is transferred between or among separate divisions, subsidiaries, or affiliates of a contractor; or

 

(8) a non-developmental item, if the procuring agency determines the item was developed exclusively at private expense and sold in substantial quantities, on a competitive basis, to multiple state and local governments.

 

A “commercially available off-the-shelf (COTS)” item -

 

(1) is any item of supply (including construction material) that is -

 

(i) a commercial item (as defined in the initial paragraph (1) of commercial item definitions);

 

(ii) sold in substantial quantities in the commercial marketplace; and

 

(iii) offered to the government, under a contract or subcontract at any tier, without modification, in the same form in which it is sold in the commercial marketplace; and

 

(2) does not include bulk cargo, as defined in section 3 of the Shipping Act of 1984 (46 U.S.C. App. 1702), such as agricultural products and petroleum products.

 

A “commercial component” means any component that is a commercial item.

B.     SERVICE CONTRACTS OF $2,500+

 

 

Federal service contractors with a contract of $2,500+ are subject to the McNamara-O’Hara Service Contract Act of 1965 (“SCA”).

 

“Service contractors” are those whose principal purpose is to provide service through the use of service employees. The SCA exempts the following:

 

1)     contracts for construction, alteration and/or repair, including painting and decorating of public buildings or public works (covered by another similar law)

 

2)     work covered by the Walsh-Healey Public Contracts Act

 

3) contracts for the carriage of freight or personnel by vessel, airplane, bus, truck, express, railway line, or oil or gas pipeline where published tariff rates are in effect

 

4) contracts for the furnishing of services by radio, telephone, telegraph, or cable companies, subject to the Communications Act of 1934

 

5) contracts for public utility services, including electric light and power, water, steam, and gas

 

6) contracts for direct services to a federal agency by an individual or individuals

 

7) post office contract stations

 

8) services performed outside of the U.S.

 

Commercially Available Services Exemptions - In addition to the 8 exemptions listed in the SCA (list above), the FAR definition of “commercial items” provides two types services as subsets of “commercial items” which are exempt under the SCA: installation, maintenance, repair or training for “commercial items”- by the type of equipment serviced, and for certain services commonly offered for other than government purposes in the commercial marketplace -by the type of service performed. Both require that the service be performed at a “catalog” or market price, be offered to the general public, and that the employees performing the service are paid the same as when performing the service for the general public. The exemption for the type of equipment serviced applies to services performed on items of equipment which are used regularly for other than government purposes, and are sold or traded by the contractor in substantial quantities to the general public in the course of normal business operations. If exempt by the type of service performed, then additionally, the employee performing the service may spend no more than 20% of their time on the service for the government, and there are additional requirements for the selection of subcontractors - that they will meet the requirements of the exemption, and either be selected based on more factors than just price or be awarded on a sole source basis. The SCA regulations list types of services generally exempt which include automobile service, travel, hotel, relocation and financial services. If subject to these exemptions, a clause describing the exemption and its qualifications should be included in all service subcontracts which help fulfill the exempt prime contract (listed in Section IV.B.2.B, herein - exempt by type of equipment serviced, exempt by type of service performed).

 

[Return to discussion of CWHSSA.]

 

The SCA requires that wages paid be the greater of: 1) minimum wage under the FLSA, 2) those paid by a predecessor contractor arrived at through collective bargaining, or 3) those amounts determined by the Department of Labor. The Wage and Hour Division of the DOL publishes the results of surveys of wages by region and by job type, available online at http://www.wdol.gov/. These prevailing wages are incorporated into federal contracts as the minimum wage for that type of work in that area, and these wages must be met or exceeded by SCA contractors. (The SCA regulations provide certain exceptions to the minimum wage requirements for apprentices, student-learners and certain handicapped workers consistent with the standards provided for in the FLSA and its regulations; as well as allowing tips to be credited toward the wage required by the contract, as long as notice is given to the employee and the employee does receive the wage required by the contract.) Additionally, the SCA requires a “fringe benefit” payment, which is a national standard for an hourly rate required to be paid in money or equivalent benefits per hour worked, up to 40 hours per week, including holidays, paid vacation and leave time, in addition to wage payments. The current dollar value of fringe benefit amounts is published online at http://www.dol.gov/whd/contracts/sca/sf98/index.asp. The benefits requirement also applies to part-time and temporary employees for hours worked. The benefit amount is not included in the hourly or regular rate when calculating the overtime premium. (The SCA does not address overtime premium rates as part of the contractor’s requirements, though they are required under the FLSA, and the Contract Work Hours and Safety Standards Act, discussed below, if the contract is for $150,000+.) Similarly, the benefit amount must be tracked separately from the hourly rate, and a contractor is required to satisfy both the wage and benefit amounts independently of each other. Most established contractors subject to the SCA utilize a third party benefits administrator (some of whom are focused on the contractor or “prevailing wage” worker market) to establish, track, pay and account for fringe benefits rather than making the cash payment. There are financial advantages to this, including the exclusion of the benefit amount from payroll taxes. Most of the fringe benefit amount is used for medical benefits and any excess is applied to retirement plan benefits. For the current national fringe benefit amount under the SCA, an employer can provide quality medical benefits, which will also satisfy as the minimum coverage requirements under the Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act (2010). In order to qualify as a “bona fide” benefit plan, the plan must constitute a legally enforceable obligation to provide benefits for death, disability, retirement, illness, medical expenses, hospitalization, or supplemental unemployment benefits, be in writing, and communicated in writing to affected employees. No benefit contributions or taxes required by other state or federal law such unemployment compensation, workers' compensation, and social security count towards the benefit payment. With prior approval from the DOL, a self-insured benefit program may qualify as a “bona fide” benefits plan based on the same criteria as a third party plan.[6]

 

Executive, administrative and professional employees are exempted from the wage and fringe benefit requirements of the SCA, but the SCA does require the contractor to pay the FLSA minimum wage amount to all employees whether working on the federal contract or not, as a condition of the contract. Fixed-price, time-and-materials, or labor-hour service contracts are subject to additional provisions governing contract pricing as a result of increases or decreases in the wage requirements during the course of the contract.[7] The SCA also requires safe working conditions, and the safety standards of the SCA are incorporated by OSHA regulations which apply to federal contractors the same as they do to any other employer.

 

The SCA provides that all contracting federal agencies include the following clauses in service contracts:

 

1. Provisions notifying the contractor of the required minimum wages and fringe benefits to be paid to employees under the contract (wage determination schedule), as well as the procedure for contracts without a wage determination schedule or a wage determination schedule that does not cover certain employees (in such cases, respectively, the employees are required to be paid according to the standards listed above, and unclassified employees are required to be fairly grouped with similarly situated employees and notice given to the contracting agency concerning these employees).

 

2. Compliance provisions notifying the contractor of its obligations (which include providing a copy of the wage determination schedule and including all provisions relating to the SCA in its subcontracts under the contract, and requiring the contractor and subcontractor to both post notices to their employees of the wage determination schedule as well as their rights under the SCA).

 

The SCA compliance clause is listed in Section IV.B.2.B, herein. Whether included in the contract or not, the SCA applies to covered contracts and subcontracts by operation of law.

 

Record-Keeping - The SCA regulations require the following records to be maintained by employers for a period of at least 3 years from the completion of work, for each employee performing work under an SCA-covered contract:

 

i.        name, address and social security number

ii.      the correct work classification or classifications, rate or rates of monetary wages paid and fringe benefits provided, rate or rates of fringe benefit payments in lieu thereof, and total daily and weekly compensation

iii.   the number of daily and weekly hours worked

iv.    any deductions, rebates or refunds from the total daily or weekly compensation

v.      a list of monetary wages and fringe benefits for those classes of service employees not included in the wage determination attached to the contract but for whom such wage rates or fringe benefits have been determined by the interested parties or by the administrator or authorized representative. A copy of the report required by the clause in paragraph (ii) of this section shall be deemed to be such a list

vi.    any list of the predecessor contractor's employees

 

Required Notice - Service contractors are required to post a notice to employees regarding their rights under the SCA - WH Publication 1313. If the contractor employs workers with disabilities under special minimum wage certificates, then it also must post a special notice for those employees - WH Publication 1284.

 

Additionally, service contractors are required to post either: the minimum wage and fringe benefits or the wage determination schedule applicable to the contract.

 

Penalties - Penalties for violating the SCA are the differences in wages and fringe benefits owed versus the amounts paid. The penalty amounts may be withheld from payments due under the contract, or the government may bring legal action to collect underpaid wages. Further, violations can result in termination of the contract (with costs of replacing the contractor being charged to the contractor) and prohibition from future government contract work for up to 3 years. Additionally, a contractor may be jointly liable for any violations of its subcontractor(s), and any of their subcontractor(s).

 

For additional information regarding compliance with the SCA wage and fringe benefits requirements and the CWHSSA overtime requirements, review the following guidance document http://www.dol.gov/whd/recovery/pwrb/Tab10SCACompliance.pdf.

 

Personal Identification Verification - Homeland Security Presidential Directive (HSPD) 12-Policy for a Common Identification Standard for Federal Employees and Contractors (2004), establishes safeguards for verifying the identity of and establishing secure identifications for federal and contractor employees who have access to government facilities or government information systems. The Directive requires that credentials only be issued: by those authorized to do so and to those whose true identity has be determined (two documents with at least one being a government issued photo id); that the person who receives the credential be the individual whose true identity has been determined; and, that the credentials be returned and/or access terminated when no longer needed by the employee for contract performance, upon such employee’s termination of employment, or upon the contractor’s completion of the contract. A compliance clause will be included in the solicitation and contract, and requires the contractor to include the clause in all subcontracts if the contract is covered by the Directive. The compliance clause is listed in Section IV.B.2.C, herein.

D.    SERVICE CONTRACTS OF $150,000+

 

1. Contract Work Hours and Safety Standards Act

 

Federal service contractors with a contract of $150,000+, and not for “commercial items” (see definition above in Section III.2.B - note in this case, “commercial services,” as a subset of “commercial items” are exempted), are also subject to the Contract Work Hours and Safety Standards Act (1962) (“CWHSSA”). Other exemptions are contracts for transportation by land, air or water; transmission of intelligence; and the purchase of supplies or materials. The CWHSSA specifically requires contractors to pay time and a half for all hours worked (on the contract at any location and requires including travel time between worksites in hours worked) over 40 hours in a week to all “laborers, mechanics, guards and watchmen,” regardless of where such work is performed, as a condition of the contract. Employees whose work is primarily clerical, supervisory or non-manual in nature do not qualify as laborers or mechanics under the CWHSSA regulations (though they may be due overtime pay under the FLSA).

 

The CWHSSA requires payment of overtime for hours worked on the covered federal contract, while the FLSA requires it for overtime hours worked in any capacity. If covered by the CWHSSA, the contract will also be subject to the SCA and the prime contract will contain a wage determination by category of employees. Under the CWHSSA, the primary problem arises from misclassifying an employee at a lower wage rate than is required, and then owing an additional amount for the difference in overtime premiums between the two rates and $10/day/employee in penalties (“liquidated damages,” - calculated by the DOL and imposed by the contracting agency). The fringe benefit, whether paid in contributions or in cash, so long as paid in full, is not used in the overtime premium calculation. For employees working at different wage rates in the same week, there are two accepted methods for calculating overtime pay. The first is averaging the hours worked at different wage rates by hours worked to determine a “regular rate” (and overtime premium); and, the alternative (which requires the employee’s consent) is paying the overtime premium for the wage rate at which the overtime hours were worked).

 

The record-keeping requirements adopt those of the SCA. The penalties include unpaid or underpaid overtime premium pay as well as fines of $10/day/employee that was unpaid or underpaid overtime premiums. The penalty amounts may be withheld from payment due under the contract, or the government may bring legal action to collect from the contractor. The CWHSSA has its own compliance clause, and a contractor is required to include this clause in any subcontract covered by the CWHSAA ($150,000+).[8] Contractors are jointly liable for any violation by any subsequent subcontractors. Additional criminal penalties apply for a willful violation. The compliance clause for the CWHSSA is located in Section IV.B.2.C, herein.

 

 

2.      Executive Order 13495 - (final regulations pending, effective date yet unknown)

 

Executive Order 13495 (2009) is not yet effective as a requirement, but will be soon. The Department of Labor has issued its final rule (discussed below), but not an effective date. EO 13495 requires successor service contractors (contract covered by the Service Contract Act, see discussion in Section III.2.B) to offer employment to those workers who performed the same services at the same facilities under the previous contract. A successor contractor and its subcontractors must offer a right of first refusal for employment to perform the same services, to the predecessor contractor’s and subcontractor’s employees, who were employed during the final month of the predecessor contract, worked exclusively on a federal contract(s), and who would be displaced from their employment by the predecessor contractor’s loss of the contract. The obligation does not apply to managerial or supervisory employees. The obligation lasts for 90 days from the first day services are performed under the successor contract, and the offer of employment must be kept open for at least 10 days. Contracts (and subcontracts even if the prime contract is subject to EO 13495) below the simplified acquisition threshold ($150,000+) are exempt from the requirements, as are contracts performed by workers who are blind or severely disabled.[9]

 

Subcontracts for $150,000+ are also subject to the Executive Order and a compliance clause for subcontracts is included in Section IV.B.2.D, herein. Successor contractors subject to the Executive Order and who discontinue any subcontract for services under the predecessor contract to perform the work directly must extend an offer of employment to predecessor subcontractor employees who would be displaced by the predecessor subcontractor’s loss of the subcontract.

 

Rules for offers and employment of predecessor contractor’s employees - Though the successor contractor will normally be given a certified list of workers qualifying for an offer, the obligation applies whether the successor contractor has been given a list of those workers or not. The successor contractor must make the offer in a language understood by the worker, and may not conduct background checks or drug screen the workers unless required by the contract. The offer may be for any position the worker is qualified for, and may be at different terms or conditions so long as the terms and conditions are not made to induce the worker to reject the offer. The successor contractor must presume, and demonstrate the effectiveness of its conclusion(s) to the contrary, that all predecessor contractor employees were service employees subject to the right to an offer, performed their job satisfactorily (requires written evidence to the contrary to justify overcoming this presumption), and were employed exclusively on a federal contract. The successor contractor may reduce the number of workers fulfilling the contract, and limit the number of workers it offers employment to accordingly, so long as, for the 90 day period of obligation, it continues to offer all available positions to workers with the right to an offer of employment. The successor contractor may choose which workers it offers positions to, so long as it is consistent with other laws and done in a way to maximize the number of possible displaced workers to employ. Additionally, a successor contractor may use any of its existing workers to perform the contract instead of the predecessor contractor’s if, the existing worker has been employed by the successor contractor for 3 months prior to the first day of services performed under the contract and the existing worker would otherwise face lay-off.

 

Recordkeeping - The contractor must maintain, for a period of at least 3 years, all offers of employment made, the basis of determinations made regarding eligibility for or preference of offers made, and the list of eligible workers provided by the predecessor contractor. The contractor is required to disclose these records to, and co-operate in any investigation by, the contracting agency or the Department of Labor.

 

Penalties - Any contractor violating the Executive Order or its regulations can be required to remedy the violation through an award of back pay to affected workers and/or hire such displaced workers, or restore certain terms or conditions of their employment. The contracting agency may withhold funds due under the contract to satisfy the remedies to affected workers.

 

Predecessor Contractor Obligations - Predecessor contractors are required to provide a list of current workers on the contract to the contracting agency 30 days prior to the contract being completed. The predecessor contractor is required to post a notice (sample notice provided in Section IV.A.7) to its workers concerning their possible rights to offers from the successor contractor under this Executive Order in English or any language common to a large number of workers who are not fluent in English.

 

 

 

3.     Ethical Conduct by Contractors and Employees

 

 

The following Laws cover a contractor’s obligations to ensure that neither the contractor, its subcontractor(s), nor the employees of either, engage in conduct which is against the ethical and social policies of the government.[10] The Laws are not enforced by any particular federal agency, but instead by the contracting agency through normal contract audit procedures, or investigations of complaints. The following Laws require, depending the amount and level of contract, employee notices and in some cases policies and educational efforts for employees. Sample employee notices are provided which define the prohibited conduct and are hyperlinked in the discussion of each Law where applicable. Policies and educational efforts should be developed by the individual contractor to meet its individual needs. Note that posting the employee notice and developing a policy or educating employees are distinct actions, and the employee notice may not fulfill the contractor’s further requirements by itself.

 

In addition to the requirements to notify a contracting agency of a violation of the Laws contained in the discussion below, federal acquisition regulations require generally that a contractor notify the contracting agency of “credible evidence of a violation of Federal criminal law involving fraud, conflict of interest, bribery, or gratuity violations found in Title 18 of the United States Code or a violation of the civil False Claims Act”[11] by the contractor, its subcontractor(s) or employees of either in connection with a government contract.

 

 

A.    Prohibition against Human Trafficking

 

 

In addition to any criminal or civil penalties imposed by other federal or state laws, the Trafficking Victims Protection Act of 2000 (as amended) prohibits every federal contractor, subcontractor or any of their employees from:

 

      1. engaging in “severe trafficking of persons,” defined as labor trafficking which is the recruitment, harboring, transportation, provision, or obtaining of a person for labor or services, through the use of force, fraud, or coercion for the purpose of subjection to involuntary servitude, peonage, debt bondage, or slavery; or, sex trafficking which is the recruitment, harboring, transportation, provision, or obtaining of a person for the purpose of a commercial sex act that is induced by force, fraud, or coercion, or in which the person induced to perform such act has not attained 18 years of age [“commercial sex act” is defined as “any sex act on account of which anything of value is given to or received by any person”]; or

 

      1. procuring a commercial sex act (whether or not it is induced by force or performed by a minor); or

 

      1. using forced labor during the time of the performance of the contract.

 

Penalties for violating the Act include cancellation of any contract, withholding payments, retaining contract award fees to or suspending or debarring the contractor from future contracts.

The Law applies to all government contracts and subcontracts of any amount. The application of this law requires five things:

 

1)     do not engage in severe trafficking of persons, procure a commercial sex act, or use forced labor

2)     use the compliance clause in all subcontracts of any type and in any amount (listed in Section IV.B.3.A, herein)

3)     notify employees of government’s policy against human trafficking and consequences to an employee who violates the policy (discipline up to and including termination of employment)

4)     penalize, terminate employment or subcontract for violation of government’s policy against human trafficking by employees and subcontractors

5)     immediately notify contracting agency or contractor above of violation by any employee, subcontractor, or subcontractor employee

 

The employee notice does not have to be a poster, and may be incorporated into an employee handbook, posted electronically or provided in any other form or manner that the employer regularly provides information to its employees, so long as all employees working on the contract receive a copy. The notice should state that as a government contractor, the contractor is subject to the federal policy and notice requirements in regards to the government’s policy against human trafficking. A sample is provided in Section IV.A.3, herein. While not required, a human trafficking awareness program implemented by a contractor will be considered as part of a good faith effort at compliance and mitigate penalties should a violation occur. Note that posting the notice and developing an awareness program are two distinct actions and the notice itself, even if providing information about how to learn more (such as the sample policy herein), while helpful, would not qualify as an awareness program. Further resources for developing an awareness program and information about how to recognize and combat human trafficking can be accessed through the U.S. State Department’s Office to Monitor and Combat Trafficking in Persons.

 

Note on Imported Products (supply contracts) Produced by Forced Child Labor. Executive Order 13126 (1999) - Prohibition of Acquisition of Products Produced by Forced or Indentured Child Labor, prohibits the use in government contracting of imported materials or products produced by forced or indentured child labor. Certain solicitations and contracts (larger contracts from developed countries are excluded, see compliance clause below) require the contractor to certify that it will not use such materials or products in fulfilling a government contract. The Department of Labor’s Bureau of International Labor Affairs (ILAB) maintains a list[12] of materials and products by country which are known to use child or forced labor. A contractor must certify that it is not providing materials or products which appear on the list, or, if providing materials or products which appear on the list, that based on a good faith effort to determine whether forced or indentured child labor was used, that it is not aware of any forced child labor being used in the materials or products for the contract.[13] A contractor agrees to cooperate with any official investigation into forced child labor. The requirement applies to contracts above the micro purchase threshold ($3,000+ supplies) including contracts for “commercial items.” Penalties for using materials or products from forced child labor or failing to cooperate with an investigation include contract cancellation and debarment from future federal contracts for up to 3 years. Though not required to include the compliance clause - FAR 52.222-19 Child Labor - Cooperation with Authorities and Remedies (Jul 2010) - in subcontracts, a contractor is liable for any use of prohibited materials or products in the contract whether provided by the contractor or a subcontractor.

 

 

B.     Prohibition against Kickbacks

 

 

The Anti-Kickback Act of 1986 prohibits any contractor, contractor employee, subcontractor or subcontractor employee from giving, receiving, offering to give, or soliciting anything of monetary value in exchange for favorable treatment in connection with a government contract or subcontract. The Law addresses kickbacks made to contractors and requires all contractors with a contract of any amount to promptly report violations to the contracting agency and requires prime contractors to co-operate with any contracting agency investigating a possible violation. It further requires prime contractors (if the contract is for $150,000+ and not for “commercial items,” see definition above in Section III.2.A) to develop internal audit procedures to prevent and detect violations. While not required of a contractor, a sample employee notice regarding kickbacks is provided in Section IV.A.4, herein which can be used as part of the measures to prevent and detect violations. Penalties include criminal enforcement against any person who knowingly violates the Law, and a civil penalty against anyone who violates the law, any contractor that person works for, as well as any contractor above (if violated by a subcontractor or subcontractor employee). A compliance clause is required in subcontracts of $150,000+ and not for “commercial items,” and is listed in Section IV.B.3.B, herein.[14]

 

Gratuities/Bribery/Procurement Integrity - Federal acquisition regulations prohibit a contractor, its agents or employees from offering or paying anything of monetary value to any government employee for favorable treatment in a government contract. Contracting employees are required to report any violation and the penalties for a violation include contract termination and contractor debarment. The compliance clause (FAR 52.203-3) applies to prime contracts of $150,000+. Criminal statutes apply for offering or paying anything of monetary value to influence any official act - up to 15 years in prison and up to $250,000 or 3 times the amount paid as a bribe. Additional civil and criminal penalties apply for obtaining competitors’ bid information prior to the awarding of a contract.

 

 

C.    Prohibition of contract funds in lobbying activities

 

 

An amendment (“Byrd Amendment”) to an appropriations bill in 1989 (Public Law 101-121) later amended by the Lobbying Disclosure Act of 1995, places two requirements on contractors as employers. First, the Byrd Amendment prohibits the use of funds “procured” from government contracts to be used to pay for any activities which attempt to influence a government official with regards to a federal contract. Second, it requires contractors to make disclosures regarding the payment of funds from any source used in lobbying activities. The Law applies to a contractor’s employees and prevents the attempt to influence any agency or congressional official to award, extend, renew or modify a government contract. Generally, the Law prohibits attempts to influence a government official with regards to a specific solicitation or contract. The Law exempts the following communications, among others, by the contractor’s employees: discussions that are not related to a specific solicitation - discussing or demonstrating the contractor’s products or services and their capabilities or technical discussions regarding the application or adaptation of the contractor’s products or services for an agency's use; and, prior to a formal solicitation - technical discussions regarding the preparation of an unsolicited proposal prior to its official submission.

 

If paying a registered lobbyist to secure government contracts, a contractor must complete a disclosure form (and update it quarterly with any changes), and a prime contractor is required to collect, retain and file with the contracting agency the disclosure forms of its subcontractors at any tier. Whether employing a lobbyist or not, a contractor, in its offer for a contract of $150,000+, is required to certify its compliance with the Byrd Amendment’s prohibition of using procured funds in lobbying activities (as set out in FAR 52.203-11), and to collect and retain certifications of compliance from its subcontractors at any tier whose subcontract is $150,000+.

 

The Byrd Amendment applies to all contracts and subcontracts of $150,000+. Violations of the prohibition of using procured funds for lobbying activities and failures to make accurate disclosures and updates to disclosures may result in civil penalties of $10,000 to $100,000 per violation. The compliance clause for this Law is in Section IV.B.3.C, herein.

 

 

D.    Drug-Free Workplaces

 

 

There is no federal law relating to all employers concerning drug-free workplaces. However, federal supply and service prime contractors who hold a single contract of $150,000+, and not for “”commercial items” (see definition above in Section III.2.A), are required to develop and implement a drug-free workplace policy as required by the Drug-Free Workplace Act of 1988.[15] The law applies to any contract fulfilled in whole or in part in the U.S. The law requires the following from contractors who are subject to it:

 

i.        publishing a statement notifying employees that the unlawful manufacture, distribution, dispensation, possession, or use of a controlled substance is prohibited in the workplace and specifying actions that will be taken against employees for violations of this prohibition

ii.      establishing a drug-free awareness program to inform employees about -

a.       the dangers of drug abuse in the workplace;

b.      the policy of maintaining a drug-free workplace;

c.       any available drug counseling, rehabilitation, or employee assistance programs; and

d.      the penalties that may be imposed upon employees for drug abuse violations

iii.   requiring that each employee engaged in the performance of such contract be given a copy of the statement required by subparagraph (i)

iv.    notifying the employee in the statement required by subparagraph (i) that as a condition of employment on such contract, the employee will -

a.       abide by the terms of the statement; and

b.      notify the employer of any criminal drug statute conviction for a violation occurring in the workplace no later than 5 days after such conviction

v.      notifying the contracting agency within 10 days after receiving notice under subparagraph (iv)(b) from an employee or otherwise receiving actual notice of such conviction

vi.    imposing a sanction on, or requiring the satisfactory participation in a drug abuse assistance or rehabilitation program by, any employee who is so convicted

vii.  making a good faith effort to continue to maintain a drug-free workplace through implementation of subparagraphs (i), (ii), (iii), (iv), (v) and (vi)

 

The employee notice does not have to be a poster, and may be incorporated into an employee handbook, posted electronically or provided in any other form or manner that the employer regularly provides information to its employees, so long as all employees working on the contract receive a copy. While not required of a subcontractor, a sample employee notice regarding drug-free workplaces is provided in Section IV.A.5, herein which can be used by any prime or subcontractor as a good faith effort at compliance with the Law and contains the information which must be used as part of a prime contractor’s ($150,000+ contract) requirement to notify employees as part of a contractor’s overall policy which must be developed by the contractor. Penalties for violating the terms of the contract relating to drug-free workplaces include suspension of payments due under the contract, termination of the contract and prohibition from future contracts for up to 5 years.

 

 

E.     Prohibition against Texting while Driving

 

 

Executive Order 13513 (2009) declares a government policy of encouraging federal contractors and subcontractors to prevent their employees from texting while driving. “Texting” includes any use of a handheld electronic device, including reading. It does not include glancing at or listening to a navigational device that is secured in a commercially designed holder affixed to the vehicle, provided that the destination and route are programmed into the device either before driving or while stopped in a location off the roadway where it is safe and legal to park. “Driving” includes while stopped at an intersection, but does not include when pulled off of the roadway, with or without the motor running.

 

Supply and service contracts of any amount may be subject to this Executive Order, depending on the agency and only if the contract is made or amended, or results from a solicitation, on or after 9/29/2010. If so, and since the Executive Order establishes a policy of “encouraging” contractors to comply, the following actions are suggested by the government: to develop, provide notice of, and enforce a policy which prohibits texting while driving on the contractor’s business, whether in a company or personal vehicle and educating employees about the dangers of texting while driving. A sample employee notice regarding texting while driving is provided in Section IV.A.6, herein, which can be used as part of a contractor’s efforts to educate employees. If the prime contract is covered by the EO, then subcontracts of $3,000+ supply/$2,500+ service - the micro-purchase thresholds are also covered, and the compliance clause is in Section IV.B.3.D, herein.

 

 

F.     “Whistleblower” Rights of Employees

 

 

The Federal Property and Administrative Services Act of 1949, as amended, prohibits a contractor from retaliating in any way against an employee who discloses “a substantial violation of law related to a contract (including the competition for or negotiation of a contract).” The Law does not provide for a private right of legal action, but allows the contracting agency to order re-instatement, back pay and costs incurred by an employee in bringing a complaint, as a remedy for the retaliation.

 

To deal with contractors providing sub-standard horses, armaments and food to the Union Army in the Civil War, the False Claims Act (“FCA”, as amended) was passed in 1863 to specifically address fraud in any government contract.[16] The law survives, and prohibits submission of false claims for payment by making or causing to be made, a claim for payment which the person “knows or has reason to know” is false; which includes a “reckless disregard” or “deliberate ignorance” of the truth or falsity of the information submitted to the government, or the omission of a material fact which creates a mistaken impression of the truth. The FCA states that no intent to defraud is required.[17] Penalties of $5,500 to $11,000 per occurrence, damages of 3 times the loss sustained by the government from the false claim, and reimbursement of costs of bringing the claim in court apply to a violator.[18] The FCA is most often used in Healthcare and Defense contracting violations, but has been used in false submissions of labor costs (violations of the Wage and Hour requirements discussed above) - compliance with such Laws is made a term or condition of the contract, and therefore asking for payment while in violation of one of the Laws, is a false assertion of compliance with the contract on which payment is based.

 

Central to the enforcement of FCA violations are “whistleblower” lawsuits. A private party can bring a Qui Tam action against the violating contractor for themselves and the government, and depending on whether the government joins the action, receive up to the 30% of the award against the violator. The FCA protects employees and subcontractors who act in furtherance “to stop 1 or more violation” of the FCA, from retaliation in any term or condition of employment or the subcontract, by allowing an award of re-instatement, twice the amount of back pay owed and attorney’s fees to bring a retaliation claim.

 

There are no employee notices[19] or compliance clauses required under the “whistleblower” Laws discussed here. However, a contractor can be liable under the FCA for violations of its subcontractor(s), which places the responsibility on the contractor to monitor the work or products and billing of its subcontractors for compliance with the terms of the prime and subcontract.

 

 

 

4.     LABOR (UNION) OBLIGATIONS

 

 

Executive Order 13496 - Federal contractors are required to post an employee notice which describes the rights all employees in the U.S. have under the National Labor Relations Act. The requirement comes from Executive Order 13496, signed in 2009. It applies to prime contracts (made or amended, or resulting from solicitations on or after 6/21/2010) above the simplified acquisition threshold ($150,000+) and related subcontracts of $10,000+. Enforcement of the order is handled by the Office of Federal Contract Compliance Programs (OFCCP, whose principal mission is to enforce three equal opportunity/affirmative action Laws which apply to federal contractors) in coordination with the DOL’s Office of Labor-Management Standards (OLMS).

 

The Executive Order requires contractors and subcontractors with covered contracts to:

 

  1. provide notice of employees’ rights under the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA). The poster can be accessed at the OLMS website.
  2. include a prescribed compliance clause in qualifying subcontracts ($10,000+) that relate to the covered contract.

 

The notice must be posted in a conspicuous location accessible to the contractor’s employees at all facilities (unless a facility not performing work on the contract is exempted in advance); and, must be posted in English and any language common to a large number of employees who are also not proficient in English. The compliance clause for Executive Order 13496 is located in Section IV.B.4.A, herein.

 

Executive Order 13494 - Also signed in 2009, this Executive Order prevents contractors from being reimbursed under a contract for expenditures related to persuading its own, or any of its affiliates’ or subsidiaries’ employees regarding forming unions or engaging in collective bargaining. Specifically, the EO applies to commercial organizations that enter into fixed-price contracts, subcontracts, and modifications to contracts and subcontracts whenever (a) cost analysis is performed, or (b) a fixed-price contract clause requires the determination or negotiation of costs. The Executive Order allows reimbursement of expenses for labor-management committees and employee educational material, so long as it does not attempt to persuade employees regarding labor rights. The FAR final rule, which became effective December 2, 2011, amends FAR 31.205-21, and there is no associated compliance clause.

 

Notice of Labor Disputes - Certain contracts contain a notice requirement to the contracting agency of actual or potential labor conflicts which will impede performance of the contract. The compliance clause FAR 52.222-1 is not required in subcontracts, though it does require the contractor to notify the contracting agency of any conflicts whether the conflict is with the contractor or a subcontractor.

 

 

 

 

5.     EQUAL OPPORTUNITY / AFFIRMATIVE ACTION OBLIGATIONS

 

 

This section discusses the obligations and requirements pertaining to equal opportunity/affirmative action that apply to all federal contractors, beginning with an overview of basic requirements and all relevant definitions of terms. This is followed by tables that summarize the qualifying conditions and requirements under each Law, followed by in-depth discussions of each Law.

 

General Equal Employment Opportunity (EEO) / Affirmative Action Obligations

 

A.    Executive Order 11246 - mandating equal employment opportunity and affirmative action obligations based on race, color, religion, sex and national origin[20]

B.     Rehabilitation Act of 1973 (Section 503) - mandating equal employment opportunity/ affirmative action and accommodation obligations regarding the mentally or physically disabled

C.     Vietnam Era Veterans’ Readjustment Assistance Act of 1974 (“VEVRAA”) - mandating equal employment opportunity/affirmative action and accommodation obligations regarding certain protected veterans

 

These Laws impose equal opportunity requirements in addition to those that you (may) already have as an employer, not in place of them. (Generally, the equal employment opportunity laws apply to all employers with 15+ employees). The contract obligations for federal contractors do not require a minimum number of employees for the basic equal opportunity/affirmative action requirements to be applicable, but they do require 50+ employees for more rigorous affirmative action, record-keeping and reporting requirements. The Laws apply to all employees and to all facilities of the contractor (unless exempted in advance) whether or not the employees or facilities are used in fulfilling the contract and the Laws do not exempt contracts for “commercial items,” “commercially available off-the-shelf items” or “commercially available services.” The Laws are administered and enforced by the Department of Labor’s Office of Federal Contract Compliance Programs (OFCCP), which coordinates with the DOL’s Veteran Employment Training and Services (VETS) for VEVRAA, and the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission for EO 11246 and Section 503.

 

Under the Laws, the OFCCP is entitled to view all records kept by the contractor with regards to compliance, and compliance with all the Laws is overseen by the OFCCP through random audits and the investigation of employee complaints of violations. If a contractor is found non-compliant, the OFCCP will enter into a “conciliation agreement” requiring the contractor to correct any deficiencies, including back pay awards or other corrective action, make-whole remedies and relief to employees. If violations are not resolved in a reasonable time and manner, legal enforcement actions are possible, which may result in penalties up to and including debarment from future contracts.

 

Basic Requirements

 

Together, the Laws require:

 

1.      compliance clauses in contracts, subcontracts and purchase orders meeting the applicable threshold amounts; the compliance clauses are different for each Law and are listed in Section IV.B, herein; solicitation clauses (representations by contractor/subcontractor) in offers or bids and annual certifications (solicitation clauses and annual certifications apply to equal opportunity, affirmative action, and veteran reports)

 

2.      notice (poster) at all covered facilities visible to all applicants (including internet applicants) and employees, of the contractor’s obligations - the required notice is what employers subject to the federal equal opportunity laws already display (EEOC-P/E-1 poster, November 2009, or the 2009 supplement if posting the earlier version of the poster) which includes the notices required under the three equal opportunity/affirmative action Laws

 

3.      the contractor to notify any union or representative of workers with whom the contractor has a collectively bargained agreement or other contract, that the contractor is both subject to (the applicable) equal opportunity/affirmative action Law(s), and is committed to taking affirmative action to employ, and advance in employment, workers with the protected characteristics of the applicable equal opportunity/affirmative action Law (Posting of EEOC-P/E-1 satisfies this requirement)

 

4.      to not segregate facilities (or not allow employees to segregate themselves) by race, color, religion, sex or national origin

 

5.      the contractor to state in all job advertising that it is “an equal opportunity employer”

 

 

6.      if subject to VEVRAA, the contractor to advertise all positions (subject to exceptions, see discussion below of VEVRAA) with a state-sponsored job bank or workforce agency

 

7.      the contractor to request self-identification of:

 

a. all applicants and employees (EO 11246) by race/ethnicity and gender, and

b. after an offer of employment, applicants (if subject to VEVRAA) as a protected veteran (see reporting requirements of VEVRAA below)

 

 

8.      the contractor to maintain all employment records including, but not limited to, job advertisements and postings; applications and resumes; and any and all expressions of interest through the Internet or related electronic data technologies as to which the contractor considered the individual for a particular position; tests and test results; interview notes; records relating to requests for reasonable accommodation; the results of any physical examination; and other records having to do with hiring, assignment, promotion, demotion, transfer, lay-off or termination, rates of pay or other terms of compensation, and selection for training or apprenticeship

 

9.      the contractor to retain all employment records for at least 1 year (or at least 2 years for larger contractors, see table below) from the date of the making of the record or the personnel action involved, whichever occurs later

 

The above requirements apply anytime a contractor is subject to the Law(s), regardless of its number of employees. Additional requirements specific to each Law are listed in the discussion of each respective Law below.

 

Definitions of Government “Contractor” and “Subcontractor”

 

The definitions of “contractors” and “subcontractors” under the equal opportunity/affirmative action Laws are broad, and meant to include any entity that holds or assists in fulfilling a government contract. Generally, a “contractor” under these Laws is one who holds a government contract for the sale, purchase or use of personal property or non-personal services. The inclusion of “sale” and “purchase” means the definition applies to contractors who sell to or purchase from the government. “Personal property” includes the lease of real property. The designation of “non-personal services” means employees of the government are not contractors (and not covered by the Laws), but essentially all other service providers, including those who employ service employees and those who provide utility, transportation, insurance or research services directly under a federal contract, are covered.[21]

 

“Subcontractors” are those who provide materials or non-personal services to the contractor that are “necessary to” fulfill the contract, and those who assume any responsibility for fulfilling the contract. The designation of “non-personal services” means that employees of contractors are not subcontractors. “Necessary to” fulfilling the contract includes all supplies or services provided to a contractor or subcontractor in directly fulfilling the contract and can extend downward indefinitely to all subcontractors who help fulfill a contract so long as the minimum coverage thresholds (amount of subcontract or purchase order) are met.

 

Additionally, affiliates, parent companies and subsidiaries of a contractor or subcontractor are covered by the equal opportunity/affirmative action Laws if meeting certain standards of common: ownership, control, management or personnel policies, and business activities.

 

Covered Contracts

 

“Covered contracts” are those exceeding the minimum dollar thresholds of each respective Law (including subcontracts if the subcontract helps fulfill the covered contract and meets the subcontract dollar thresholds of each respective Law). Coverage is assumed unless the government agency believes the annual contract will not meet the annual dollar thresholds. That determination must be reviewed annually under any contract, and at any time a single order or purchase exceeds the dollar threshold, the contract will be and will remain covered by the applicable Law(s) thereafter.

 

Who/What is Covered

 

Generally, if a contractor is subject to the equal opportunity/affirmative action Law(s), the requirements apply to its treatment of all applicants and employees and at all of the contractor’s facilities. In no cases do the requirements apply to contracts that affect national security or to employees who work and were recruited outside of the U.S. and its territories. Additionally, in certain cases coverage may be waived in advance by the OFCCP for specific contracts (by subject matter - when in the national interest), or for groups of employees and facilities not involved in the fulfillment of the contract.

 

Anti-Discrimination

 

The actions taken by a contractor regarding covered employees may not discriminate in any term or condition of employment, including recruitment, hiring, assignment, selection for training, advancement, compensation or benefits, based on the protected characteristics listed in the Laws - race, color, religion, sex, national origin, disability and protected veteran status. Discrimination may be shown by intentionally different treatment of individuals with any of the characteristics protected by the equal opportunity/affirmative action Laws, as well as disparate impact resulting from a policy that appears neutral but has a disproportionate impact on individuals with one or more of the protected characteristics. Section 503 and VEVRAA allow giving consideration to or preference to disabled or veteran applicants/employees as not being disabled or not being a veteran are not protected characteristics. However, in giving such preference to a disabled or veteran applicant/employee, a contractor must not violate the requirements of EO 11246. For example, if veteran applicants/employees are overwhelmingly male, a disparate impact violation of EO 11246 is not justified by giving such preference.

 

Retaliation

 

A contractor is prohibited from retaliating against an employee in any term or condition of employment who objects to an adverse employment action on grounds of violation of any equal opportunity/affirmative action Law, for themselves or another applicant/employee, or files or assists in an investigation or a charge of discrimination.

 

Affirmative Action

 

As defined by the Laws and regulations applicable to federal contractors, “affirmative action” requirements are a group of affirmative actions a contractor must take (job advertisement requirements, employee notices, prevention of segregated facilities, data collection and record-keeping, as examples) in addition to not discriminating. It does not require giving preference to any group or meeting any quotas. It does include taking steps to reach out to individuals with the protected characteristics. Affirmative action includes, but is not limited to the following: employment, upgrading, demotion, or transfer, recruitment or recruitment advertising; layoff or termination; rates of pay or other forms of compensation; and selection for training, including apprenticeship. All contractors covered under these Laws must undertake these affirmative actions as a condition of the contract. Larger employer contractors face additional “affirmative action program” (“AAP”) requirements beyond the basic requirements, which are discussed briefly herein.

 

Conditional Requirements

 

When a contractor meets a threshold number of employees (50+) and/or a certain value of government contracts or subcontracts ($50,000+) additional requirements include reporting workforce composition (EEO-1, VETS-100A - if subject to VEVRAA regardless of number of employees), and initiating a written Affirmative Action Program, or AAP.[22] An AAP is a collective and comprehensive series of proactive internal audits and procedures designed to ensure equal employment opportunity, by identifying and resolving barriers to equal employment opportunity where they exist. AAPs and their disparate impact analyses involve a high degree of data collection and statistical analysis. Preparation and implementation of an AAP should be guided by legal counsel, a consulting company that specializes in their preparation, or the OFCCP. Discussion of Each Law

 

 

A. Executive Order 11246 (issued 1965)

 

The intent of EO 11246 is “insuring of equal opportunity for all persons, without regard to race, color, religion, sex, or national origin, employed or seeking employment with Government contractors.” This Executive Order requires contractors and subcontractors with any combination of prime contracts or related subcontracts or purchase orders (aggregated) totaling $10,000+ in any rolling 12-month period to:

 

  1. Include specified equal opportunity/affirmative action and segregated facilities compliance clauses in all subcontracts relating to covered contracts. (Compliance clauses located in Section IV.B.5.A, herein.)
  2. Refrain from discrimination and engage in affirmative action to ensure that applicants and employees receive equal employment opportunity regardless of race, color, religion, sex, or national origin. (Note that “affirmative action” does not mean giving preferences to any group, and involves less than preparing and implementing an AAP as required of larger employer contractors.)
  3. Posting EEOC-P/E-1 (“equal opportunity is the law,” November 2009, or the 2009 supplement if posting the earlier version of the poster) notice in a conspicuous place at all covered facilities visible to all applicants (including internet applicants) and employees. The poster is only required to be posted in English.
  4. Advertising with the following “equal opportunity” clause: “all qualified applicants will receive consideration for employment without regard to race, color, religion, sex or national origin” or, identifying the contractor as “an equal opportunity employer.”
  5. Collect and maintain race/ethnicity and gender information from all applicants and employees (see discussion below).
  6. Validate selection procedures for employment, training, advancement and reductions in force to ensure that the procedures do not produce a disparate impact on applicants/employees with protected characteristics.
  7. Refrain from segregating work facilities on the basis of race, color, religion, sex or national origin, and not allow employees to segregate themselves based on any of the same criteria. All common and work areas and facilities must be non-segregated; but, for privacy between the sexes in certain areas, a contractor must provide segregated or single-user restrooms and washrooms and segregated dressing and sleeping areas (if applicable).

 

 

Coverage - In no cases do the requirements apply to contracts that affect national security or to employees who work and were recruited outside of the U.S. (and its territories). In certain cases, coverage may be waived for specific contracts based on subject matter, when any contracting government agency feels it is in the national interest, or, with approval in advance by the OFCCP, for groups of employees or facilities not involved in the fulfillment of the contract. Certain other exemptions exist for religious organizations to give preference to members of that religion, and for contractors fulfilling contracts close to Indian reservations in which the contractor provides employment preference to residents of the reservation.

 

Union Notice - Contractors subject to EO 11246 are required to notify any union or representative of workers with which the contractor has a collectively bargained agreement or other contract, that the contractor is both subject to EO 11246 and is committed to taking affirmative action to employ, and advance in employment, female and minority individuals. The OFCCP recognizes the posting of the combined “equal opportunity is the law” poster (EEOC-P/E-1, November 2009, or the 2009 supplement if posting the earlier version of the poster) in the workplace in a location accessible and visible to all employees working on the contract as meeting the union notice requirement.

 

Anti-Discrimination - EO 11246 prohibits discrimination on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, or national origin in the following matters: employment, upgrading, demotion, or transfer; recruitment or recruitment advertising; layoff or termination; rates of pay or other forms of compensation; and selection for training, including apprenticeship. Discrimination may be shown by intentional different treatment of individuals with any of the characteristics protected by the equal opportunity/affirmative action Laws, as well as disparate impact resulting from a policy that appears neutral but has a disproportionate impact on individuals with one or more of the protected characteristics.

 

Record-Keeping Requirements - EO 11246 requires a contractor to maintain all employment records including, but not limited to, job advertisements and postings; applications and resumes; any and all expressions of interest through the Internet or related electronic data technologies as to which the contractor considered the individual for a particular position; tests and test results; interview notes; the results of any physical examination; and other records having to do with hiring, assignment, promotion, demotion, transfer, lay-off or termination, rates of pay or other terms of compensation, and selection for training or apprenticeship. A contractor must retain all employment records for at least 1 year (or at least 2 years for larger contractors, see table above) from the date of the making of the record or the personnel action involved, whichever occurs later. Additionally, a contractor should keep written records of correspondence with referral organizations and any other efforts made to reach out to minority or female applicants, as well as a log of all applicants (can be incorporated into any other applicant/employee flow log) identifying the applicant’s race and gender.

 

The purpose of the record-keeping requirements is to provide the OFCCP (and contractor) with the data necessary to determine whether a contractor’s employment decisions and processes are non-discriminatory. With regards to EO 11246, and regardless of its number of employees, a contractor is required to identify and maintain the records for the race/ethnicity and gender of every employee and, where possible, every applicant. The preferred method is an invitation to self-identify, which must state that the applicant or employee is not obligated to provide the information. The self-identifying information must be maintained separately from the application or personnel file, using a “tear-off” part of an application or a separate form. In addition to stating the voluntary nature of the response, the form should explain that the information is gathered by the contractor to comply with government-required data collection, that neither failure to respond nor any information given will be considered in employment decisions, and that the form and information will be maintained separately.

 

The information must be requested and if provided, maintained for each applicant and employee, and must be identifiable for each applicant and employee. This requirement creates a practical problem: a contractor cannot consider race/ethnicity or gender in employment decisions, but must maintain records for each applicant and employee identifying these characteristics. To balance these competing interests, a procedure can be used in which an employee of the contractor who does not make employment decisions has exclusive access to the information linking the demographic information to the applicant or employee. By using a numbering system for applications that correspond to numbers on a “tear-off” sheet, or separate form, a contractor can separate the data collection and employment decision making functions, fulfill its promise to applicants and employees not to consider their information in employment decisions, protect the privacy of the individuals, and avoid the appearance of considering protected characteristics.

 

In the absence of self-identification, referring to other employment records and visual observation are acceptable methods of compiling the information (though guessing, particularly about race/ethnicity is not required). If an applicant or employee refuses to self-identify and no other records are available, the OFCCP will not find a violation for failure to collect the data. Unless subject to the EEO-1 requirements (discussed below), this data is not required to be filed, but must be shown to the OFCCP upon request. Further, the record-keeping obligations belong to the contractor whether or not the contractor uses an outside agency to recruit or screen applicants. The current racial/ethnic categories are Hispanic or Latino, White (not Hispanic or Latino), Black or African American (not Hispanic or Latino), Native Hawaiian or Other Pacific Islander (not Hispanic or Latino), Asian (not Hispanic or Latino), American Indian or Alaska Native (not Hispanic or Latino), and Two or More Races (not Hispanic or Latino).[23] A sample invitation to self-identify is attached in Section IV.A.1 of this document.

 

Internet Applicants - The regulations defining employment decision-making processes that must be followed come from the “Uniform Guidelines on Employee Selection Procedures” (UGESP). These standards were developed in 1978 by several federal agencies, including the OFCCP and EEOC, to provide guidance to employers subject to EO 11246 (government contractors) and Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 (essentially all employers with 15+ employees). The UGESP apply to selection procedures used as a basis for any employment decision. Specifically, the UGESP require that a contractor maintain and have available for inspection, records or other information that disclose the impact its selection procedures have upon employment opportunities of persons by race/ethnicity and gender. In 2004, the same agencies supplemented the UGESP to address the record-keeping obligations as affected by technological developments in recruiting and hiring; and, to further define when the obligation to collect race/ethnicity and gender applies whether an internet applicant or not. The Internet Applicant Rule (“the rule”) applies to all employment and promotion requests and searches, and applies by position being filled - if using electronic submissions, the rule applies to all candidates regardless of the manner of submission. It primarily affects larger employer contractors who process large volumes of resumes and applications. For contractors only subject to the basic requirements, the significance of the rule is that if accepting submissions electronically, then for any individual (whether submitting electronically or not) a contractor “considers” for a particular position (judges the qualifications of the individual) the contractor is required to solicit information and maintain records identifying the race/ethnicity and gender of applicants and employees. An “applicant” is an individual who meets the basic qualifications of the job and at no time removes himself or herself from consideration for the job - two or more unanswered requests for confirmation of interest is considered a removal from consideration. Again, procedures to maintain the demographic information for each applicant and to separate it from the hiring process are required.

 

The rule also requires that the basic qualifications of a position be established in advance, may not be altered during the selection process; and, the basic qualifications must contain/be:

 

i.        non-comparative features of a job seeker (e.g., three years' experience in a particular position, rather than a comparative requirement such as being one of the top five among the candidates in years of experience)

ii.      objective (e.g., a Bachelor's degree in accounting, but not a technical degree from a “good” school)

iii.   relevant to performance of the particular position

 

The rule further states that for any search of internal or external databases, a contractor is required to maintain the date, position being filled, basic qualifications, search criteria, and, if the search produces individuals selected by the contractor, then the individuals produced from the search. A contractor is further required to maintain records of all candidates the contractor attempted to contact regarding their interest in the position. For internal databases, a contractor must maintain the record of dates of adding each resume to its internal database.

 

The UGESP and the Internet Applicant rule place requirements on contractors to be deliberate in its hiring and promotion practices, and to be able to replicate the process by which applicants and employees are informed, screened and selected for openings. Developing the qualifications for applicants, a process for searching, and properly maintaining records of applicants and the process are all necessary to fulfill the affirmative action responsibilities required by EO 11246. For further information on the UGESP and internet applicant rule see http://www.uniformguidelines.com/ and http://www.dol.gov/ofccp/regs/compliance/faqs/iappfaqs.htm.

 

 

Religious Accommodation - A contractor subject to EO 11246 must also conform in its employment practices to the “Guidelines for Discrimination Based on Religion or National Origin,” which are intended to protect individuals of Eastern, Middle and Southern European origin, particularly Jews, Catholics, Italians, Greeks and Slavic groups, who at the time of the guidelines’ publication (1978) were believed to be under-represented in mid- and upper-level management positions. The affirmative action requirements include: internally - communicating a contractor’s commitment not to discriminate among employees, fostering a workplace environment that promotes non-discrimination, and reviewing the contractor’s processes to ensure that the obligation to provide equal employment opportunity without regard to religion or national origin is being fully implemented; and externally - communicating the contractor’s commitment to provide equal employment opportunity without regard to religion or national origin to all recruitment sources. Finally, a contractor is required to make reasonable accommodation for employees’ religious observances, so long as this practice does not cause “undue hardship” for the contractor’s business.

 

Gender Equality - The regulations for EO 11246 also detail the special considerations required to ensure that male and female employees are treated equally in the workplace. Also developed in 1978, the gender considerations require the following:

 

i.        to not make any distinction based upon gender in employment opportunities, wages, hours, or other conditions of employment

ii.      to not make any distinction between married and unmarried persons of one gender that is not made between married and unmarried persons of the opposite gender

iii.    to ensure appropriate physical facilities for both genders; and to not refuse to hire men or women, or deny men or women a particular job, because there are no restroom or associated facilities; unless the contractor is able to show that the construction of the facilities would be unreasonable for such reasons as excessive expense or lack of space

iv.     to not deny a female employee the right to any job that she is qualified to perform in reliance upon a State “protective” law

v.       following childbirth, and upon signifying her intent to return within a reasonable time, a female employee must be re-instated to her original job or to a position of like status and pay, without loss of service credits

vi.     to not specify any differences for male and female employees in either mandatory or optional retirement age

vii.  to not base wage decisions, schedules, training program eligibility or job classifications on the gender of the employee

viii. to take affirmative action to recruit women to apply for those jobs from which they have previously been excluded

 

Note that equal pay for equal work between genders is a priority of the Obama administration, and the OFCCP is taking a leadership role in implementing the administration’s objectives. The statistics show that wages earned by female workers, while greatly improved over the last 50 years, still falls short of those for male workers. The agency asks employers to consider the effects of an hourly wage differential over a 40+ year career. If not for moral reasons, then for practical ones, for a contractor of any size, a pro-active effort at evaluating possible wage disparities by gender (if not by race/ethnicity as well) is an important consideration at this time. For further information, see discussion entitled “additional resources” in Section III.5, below. As with records required by the UGESP and internet applicant rule, records of efforts made by a contractor to comply with the rules regarding religious accommodation and gender equity should be maintained for at least the applicable 1 year (or 2 year depending on size - see charts above) period.

 

Reporting Requirements - Contractors with 50+ employees and any single Prime contract or related 1st Tier subcontract or purchase order in the amount of $50,000+ are required to file with the EEO-1 Joint Reporting Committee: Form EEO-1 (which details employees by category of work performed and identifies each worker in each category by race/ethnicity and gender). The EEO-1 is required to be filed by September 30th annually (and contain workforce figures from any pay period from July through September of the current year) so long as the threshold amounts are met, and/or within 30 days of the qualifying event that first subjects the contractor to the filing requirement (i.e., obtaining the contract/subcontract/ purchase order or reaching 50+ employees). For instructions, and to file electronically, access the EEOC/EEO-1 page.

 

Affirmative Action Program - Contractors with 50+ employees and any single contract or related subcontract or purchase order in the amount of $50,000+ are required to prepare an AAP that describes the policies, practices and procedures the contractor uses to ensure that all qualified applicants and employees receive equal opportunities for employment and advancement; and, if the contractor is not employing women or minorities at a rate to be expected given their availability in the relevant labor market, the AAP must include specific practical steps to address the issue. The AAP is required to be updated annually if the threshold conditions are still met, and must be shown to the OFCCP upon request. There is no employee notice requirement for applicants/employees regarding an AAP under EO 11246. Affirmative action required for an AAP does not include giving preference to a particular group or initiating a quota system, but does include taking steps to advertise for, and to hire, a diverse workforce, including utilizing a state-sponsored job bank or workforce agency for employee referrals. If required to prepare an AAP, it is prudent to seek assistance from legal counsel, an AAP consulting service, or the OFCCP.

 

 

B. Rehabilitation Act of 1973 (Section 503)

 

 

This Law requires governmental contractors and subcontractors with a prime contract or any related subcontract or purchase order totaling $15,000+ to:

 

  1. Include a prescribed equal opportunity/affirmative action compliance clause in all of its subcontracts that relate to the covered contract. (Compliance clause located in Section IV.B.5.B, herein.)
  2. Not discriminate against disabled applicants/employees, and to take affirmative action to recruit, hire, promote and accommodate physically or mentally disabled applicants/employees, including if using an exclusively on-line application system, making it accessible to the visually impaired.
  3. Posting EEOC-P/E-1 (“equal opportunity is the law”) notice in a conspicuous place at all covered facilities visible to all applicants (including internet applicants) and employees. The poster is only required to be posted in English.
  4. Advertising for employment as “an equal opportunity employer.”

 

Unless subject to the AAP requirements (discussed below), the employee notice requirements are met by posting the “equal opportunity is the law” poster (EEOC-P/E-1, November 2009, or the 2009 supplement if posting the earlier version of the poster).

 

The substantive protections afforded an applicant/employee under this Law are essentially the same as under the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 (ADA), and are applied to all federal contractors regardless of number of employees (the ADA does not apply to employers with fewer than 15 employees). An applicant can be asked if they are capable of performing the physical requirements of a job and may be asked to demonstrate this capability. Medical exams are allowed after an offer of employment but only if they are required for all other newly-hired employees. A reasonable accommodation of the workplace is required to comply with the anti-discrimination requirements, and unless an applicant/employee is unable to perform a significant part of the job, or poses a “direct threat” to the safety of himself/herself or others, then the applicant/employee must be accommodated. To make complying with the accommodation requirements easier on contractors, federal and state agencies offer financial, training and assistance resources - see discussion entitled “additional resources” in Section III.5, below.

 

Union Notice - Contractors subject to Section 503 are required to notify any union or representative of workers with which the contractor has a collectively bargained agreement or other contract, that the contractor is both subject to Section 503 and committed to taking affirmative action to employ, accommodate, and advance in employment, mentally or physically disabled individuals. The OFCCP recognizes the posting of the combined “equal opportunity is the law” poster (EEOC-P/E-1, November 2009, or the 2009 supplement if posting the earlier version of the poster) at all covered facilities in a location accessible and visible to all employees working on the contract as meeting the union notice requirement.

 

Anti-Discrimination - Section 503 prohibits discrimination on the basis of physical or mental disability in the following matters: recruitment, advertising and job application procedures; hiring, upgrading, promotion, award of tenure, demotion, transfer, layoff, termination, right of return from layoff, and rehiring; rates of pay or any other form of compensation and changes in compensation; job assignments, job classifications, organizational structures, position descriptions, lines of progression, and seniority lists; leaves of absence, sick leave, or any other leave; fringe benefits available by virtue of employment, whether or not administered by the contractor; selection and financial support for training, including apprenticeship, professional meetings, conferences, and other related activities and selection for leaves of absence to pursue training; activities sponsored by the contractor including social or recreational programs; or any other term, condition or privilege of employment.

 

Record-Keeping Requirements - Section 503 requires a contractor to maintain all employment records including, but not limited to, job advertisements and postings; applications and resumes; any and all expressions of interest through the Internet or related electronic data technologies as to which the contractor considered the individual for a particular position; tests and test results; interview notes; records relating to requests for reasonable accommodation including medical and maternity leaves; the results of any physical examination; and other records having to do with hiring, assignment, promotion, demotion, transfer, lay-off or termination, rates of pay or other terms of compensation, and selection for training or apprenticeship. A contractor must retain all employment records for at least 1 year (or at least 2 years for larger contractors, see table above) from the date of the making of the record or the personnel action involved, whichever occurs later. Additionally, with regards to disabled applicants, a contractor should keep written records of correspondence with referral organizations and any other efforts made to reach out to disabled applicants, as well as a log of disabled applicants (can be incorporated into any other applicant/employee flow log) and all requests and responses for accommodation by disabled applicants/employees including records of medical and maternity leaves and return to work assignments.

 

Affirmative Action Program - Contractors with 50+ employees and any single contract or related subcontract or purchase order in the amount of $50,000+ are required to:

 

  1. develop an AAP for disabled applicants/employees and update it annually
  2. make the AAP available to the OFCCP upon request
  3. publish notice in all covered facilities to notify applicants/employees of the AAP, its location and the hours at which it may be accessed by those parties
  4. invite newly-hired employees and existing employees not previously asked (and only during a review of personnel characteristics for other purposes) to self-identify themselves as disabled in order to receive any affirmative action considerations
  5. communicate its policy statement from the AAP to its subcontractors, vendors and suppliers and request they undertake similar action

  7. conduct outreach to groups likely to help in the recruitment and advancement of disabled employees

 

 

C. Vietnam Era Veterans’ Readjustment Assistance Act of 1974 (VEVRAA)

 

 

VEVRAA requires contractors and subcontractors with a prime contract or any related subcontract or purchase order totaling $100,000+ and only if the prime contract was made or amended on or after 12/1/2003 ($25,000+ is the coverage threshold for prime contract and related subcontract or purchase order made before 12/1/2003 and not since amended) to:

 

  1. Include prescribed equal opportunity/affirmative action and veterans reporting compliance clauses in all of its subcontracts that relate to the covered contract. (Compliance clauses located in Section IV.B.5.C, herein.)
  2. Not discriminate against, and to take affirmative action to recruit, hire and promote protected veterans and to accommodate disabled military veteran applicants/employees.
  3. Posting EEOC-P/E-1 (“equal opportunity is the law”) notice in a conspicuous place at all covered facilities visible to all applicants (including internet applicants) and employees. The poster is only required to be posted in English.
  4. Advertise for employees with a state-sponsored job bank or workforce agency (see requirements below) and identify the contractor as “an equal opportunity employer.”
  5. Invite applicants (post offer of employment) to self-identify as a protected veteran.
  6. Annually, file VETS-100A report.

 

Veteran means a person who served in the active military, naval, or air service of the United States, and who was discharged or released under conditions other than dishonorable. The categories of veterans subject to the affirmative action requirements are:

 

disabled veteran - a veteran of the U.S. military, ground, naval or air service who is entitled to compensation (or who but for the receipt of military retired pay would be entitled to compensation) under laws administered by the Secretary of Veterans Affairs, or a person who was discharged or released from active duty because of a service-connected disability;

other protected veterans - who served on active duty during a war or in a campaign or expedition for which a campaign badge has been authorized by the DOD;

Armed Forces service medal veteran - any veteran who, while serving on active duty in the U.S. military, ground, naval or air service, participated in a United States military operation for which an Armed Forces service medal was awarded pursuant to Executive Order 12985; and

recently separated veteran - any veteran during the three-year period beginning on the date of such veteran's discharge or release from active duty in the U.S. military, ground, naval or air service;

 

Unless subject to the AAP requirements (discussed below), the employee notice requirements are met by posting the “equal opportunity is the law” poster (EEOC-P/E-1, November 2009, or the 2009 supplement if posting the earlier version of the poster).

 

The substantive protections afforded an applicant/employee under this Law include hiring, promoting and compensating without discrimination based on protected veteran status; and, meeting the same requirements for mentally or physically disabled veterans as Section 503 - an applicant can be asked if they are capable of performing the physical requirements of a job and may be asked to demonstrate this capability. Medical exams are allowed after an offer of employment but only if they are required for all other newly-hired employees. A reasonable accommodation of the workplace is required to comply with the anti-discrimination requirements, and unless an applicant/employee is unable to perform a significant part of the job, or poses a “direct threat” to the safety of himself/herself or others, then the applicant/employee must be accommodated. To make complying with the accommodation requirements easier on contractors, federal and state agencies offer financial, training and assistance resources - see discussion entitled “additional resources” in Section III.5, below.

 

Union Notice - Contractors subject to VEVRAA are required to notify any union or representative of workers with which the contractor has a collectively bargained agreement or other contract, that the contractor is both subject to VEVRAA and committed to taking affirmative action to employ, and advance in employment, protected veterans. The OFCCP recognizes the posting of the combined “equal opportunity is the law” poster (EEOC-P/E-1, November 2009, or the 2009 supplement if posting the earlier version of the poster) at all covered facilities in a location accessible and visible to all employees working on the contract as meeting the union notice requirement.

 

Advertising Requirements - Contractors subject to VEVRAA are required to advertise with a state-sponsored job bank or workface agency for applicants, and must maintain records showing compliance with this requirement. Your state’s workforce agency can advise you on the location or office to post the advertisements. Additionally, the DOL sponsors a career website that lists job banks by state: http://www.jobbankinfo.org/. The advertisement should state that the contractor is an “equal opportunity employer.” Advertisements may also be made with a private agency or service, so long as the state-sponsored job bank or state workforce agency advertisement is made concurrently. Jobs exempt from this requirement are management positions, positions filled from within the organization, and jobs lasting 3 days or less.

 

Anti-Discrimination - The specific issues raised by VEVRAA regulations prohibit discrimination on the basis of protected veteran status in the following matters: recruitment, advertising and job application procedures; hiring, upgrading, promotion, award of tenure, demotion, transfer, layoff, termination, right of return from layoff, and rehiring; rates of pay or any other form of compensation and changes in compensation; job assignments, job classifications, organizational structures, position descriptions, lines of progression, and seniority lists; leaves of absence, sick leave, or any other leave; fringe benefits available by virtue of employment, whether or not administered by the contractor; selection and financial support for training, including apprenticeship, professional meetings, conferences, and other related activities; selection for leaves of absence to pursue training; activities sponsored by the contractor including social or recreational programs; or any other term, condition or privilege of employment.

 

Reporting Requirements - Regardless of its number of employees, if a contractor has any single contract, subcontract or purchase order in the amount of $100,000+, the following is required to be filed with DOL - Veteran Employment Training Service (VETS): Form VETS-100A listing protected veterans employed by the contractor. (For contracts made before December 1, 2003 and not since amended, the coverage threshold is $25,000 and Form VETS-100 is filed.) The form requires the contractor to list:

 

        1. the total number of employees in the workforce of the contractor, by job category and hiring location, and the number of such employees by job category and hiring location, who are disabled veterans, other protected veterans, Armed Forces service medal veterans, and recently separated veterans
        2. the total number of new employees hired by the contractor or subcontractor during the period covered by the report, and of such employees, the number who are disabled veterans, other protected veterans, Armed Forces service medal veterans, and recently separated veterans
        3. the maximum number and minimum number of employees of the contractor or subcontractor at each hiring location during the period covered by the report

 

If employing 50+ employees, a contractor subject to VEVRAA will by default be subject to its AAP requirements, which, among other things, require an invitation to self-identify as a protected veteran (after an offer of employment) to avail the employee of the AAP benefits.[24] When employing less than 50 employees, a contractor with a contract or any related subcontract or purchase order totaling $100,000+ is still required to file the VETS-100A, reporting on the number of employees and new hires since the last report who are protected veterans. To compile this information, a contractor must invite employees to self-identify in the same way it would if subject to the AAP requirements, but without promising a benefit from an AAP (which may not exist for that contractor). In either case, a separate invitation for self-identification should be used with applicants (after an offer of employment), and the completed forms should be maintained separately from the personnel file. Discussion of Application and Enforcement of Equal Opportunity / Affirmative Action Laws

 

Contractor’s Use of Equal Opportunity/Affirmative Action Compliance Clauses in Subcontracts - As discussed, a contractor is required to include the applicable compliance clause(s) in all of its subcontracts and purchase orders if those subcontracts or purchase orders are above the dollar amount coverage thresholds for subcontracts under the respective Law(s). Further, the regulations for the Laws provide that the clauses are incorporated by operation of law into each covered contract and subcontract whether referenced in the contract or not. The compliance clauses also require the subcontractor to further require any of its subcontractors, if the subcontract or purchase order is above the dollar amount coverage thresholds for subcontracts under the respective Law(s), to comply with the notice and contract provisions, and so on indefinitely. A good faith effort at compliance would entail at least using the required compliance clause(s) and (if/when possible) pointing out to subcontractors their further obligations under these Laws. Many larger contractors require their subcontractors to attend training on the equal opportunity/affirmative action policies of the contractor and take other pro-active measures to ensure compliance with these Laws by their subcontractors.

 

Audit Process Changes in 2011 - Beginning in January 2011, the OFCCP re-designated its compliance review process for Supply and Service contractors and changed the standards under which a more thorough compliance evaluation can be initiated. By lowering the standards under which violations may be determined through a desk audit, the OFCCP has opened up virtually any desk-audited contractor to a complete compliance evaluation, including an on-site audit. Traditionally, the OFCCP has focused its resources on uncovering and correcting discrimination, and typically larger scale or systemic discrimination. The focus of the OFCCP is now equally on addressing affirmative action obligations of contractors, which increases the necessity for contractors to understand and comply with the affirmative action requirements discussed herein.

 

If a contractor is selected for any level of compliance evaluation (or receives any notice from the OFCCP singling it out), it is prudent to seek legal counsel. When reaching the thresholds requiring an AAP, it is equally advisable to seek legal counsel or, in the alternative, a consulting company specializing in data collection/management for compliance with government contractor AAP requirements.

The ILG facilitates education and interaction between the OFCCP and federal contractors. There are regional ILGs as well, which can be found at the national ILG website listed above. The groups meet regularly to provide education and discussion opportunities for and among contractors to facilitate compliance with the contractor equal opportunity/affirmative action Laws. The efforts made by contractors to honor the intent of these Laws, display some of the best examples in America, of promoting equality and human dignity in the workplace. For a smaller or new government contractor, the regional ILGs provide an opportunity to learn how larger contractors with greater resources or more experience, undertake the implementation of these Laws. When preparing subcontracts, it might be feasible to identify and cite the compliance clauses applicable to that subcontract, but in purchase orders, it isn’t. The procedure and language for reference and inclusion of compliance clauses should be prepared with the assistance of legal counsel. ARRA

 

In addition to other reporting requirements specific to contracts which are funded in part or in whole by ARRA funds, ARRA requires four things related to contractors as employers. A brief discussion of the requirements is followed by the required “whistleblower” compliance clause as well as the optional reporting requirements compliance clause, and citations for inclusion of both in subcontracts by reference.

 

Requirements:

 

For all contractors and subcontractors:

 

1.      a compliance clause regarding employee “whistleblower” protections under ARRA

2.      employee notice (Poster) of “whistleblower” protections under ARRA

 

Quarterly Reporting Requirements:

 

For all prime contractors:

 

3.      reporting of jobs created and retained through the use of ARRA funds

 

For all prime contractors, to report under certain conditions, their own and 1st tier subcontractors’:

 

4.      compensation of 5 highest paid executives

 

ARRA provides its own “whistleblower” protections for ARRA contract employees. The Law prohibits contractors from retaliating against employees who report to a contracting agency, other state or federal regulatory agency or the employee’s supervisor: gross mismanagement of ARRA funds, a danger to the public health or safety by use of ARRA funds, or any other violation of law, rule or regulation applicable to the contract. ARRA provides for a contracting agency to investigate the alleged retaliation, order re-instatement, back pay and attorney’s fees, and further provides a right for the employee to file a civil action for the same remedies, if not already met, and the contracting agency fails to do so within a certain period of time.

 

The required compliance clause states that a contractor agrees to post notice (Poster) of its employees’ “whistleblower” protections under ARRA, and must be included in subcontracts of any amount which are funded in part or in whole with ARRA funds.

 

Quarterly Reporting. Prime contractors are required to report quarterly on the use of ARRA funds including, “jobs created” and “jobs retained” through the use of ARRA contracts and executive compensation of certain contractors and 1st tier subcontractors. “Jobs created” means an estimate of those new positions created and filled, or previously existing unfilled positions that are filled, as a result of funding by ARRA, and reported as “full-time equivalents,” - all hours worked divided by the total number of hours in a full-time schedule, as defined by the contractor. “Jobs retained” means an estimate of those previously existing filled positions that are retained as a result of funding by ARRA, also reported as full-time equivalents.

 

A prime contractor is additionally required to report quarterly the total compensation (cash and noncash value) of its 5 highest paid executives in the year in which the contract was awarded if, in the contractor’s previous fiscal year, it received $25,000,000+ and at least 80 percent of its annual gross revenues from federal contracts and subcontracts, and the information has not already been submitted as required by the SEC or IRS. "Executive" means officers, managing partners, or any other employees in management positions. A prime contractor must also submit the same information for its 1st tier subcontractors who hold a subcontract of $25,000+ and in the 1st tier subcontractor’s previous fiscal year received $25,000,000+ and at least 80 percent of its annual gross revenues from federal contracts and subcontracts. "First-tier subcontract" means a contract awarded directly by a prime contractor whose contract is funded by ARRA, to furnish supplies or services for performance of a prime contract, but excludes supplier agreements with vendors, such as long-term arrangements for materials or supplies that would normally be applied to a contractor's general and administrative expenses or indirect cost.

 

Reporting is made at http://www.federalreporting.gov/, and appears to the public at http://www.recovery.gov/. Quarterly reports under ARRA are due the 10th day of the first month following the end of each calendar quarter (January 10th, April 10th, July 10th, October 10th). Further information regarding the quarterly reporting requirements can be accessed at the Office of Management and Budget’s FAQ regarding ARRA reporting.
